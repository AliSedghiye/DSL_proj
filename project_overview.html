01

Data Loading & Preprocessing

notebooks/01_data_loading.ipynb

Ingests the raw Bank of Italy IACOFI 2023 survey file and prepares it for downstream analysis. The survey uses coded missing-value sentinels rather than true NaNs, which must be replaced before any computation.

Steps

  • Load Database_ENG.csv via pandas — 4,862 rows × 219 columns
  • Inspect column names, dtypes (116 int64, 103 float64), and top-missing columns (many "follow-up only" items have 4,332+ NaNs)
  • Replace sentinel codes -97 (don't know), -98 (not applicable), -99 (refused), -999 (irrelevant) with np.nan
  • Enrich with ISTAT 2023 macro-regional statistics (unemployment rate & internet penetration) merged on region code qd2
  • Output: data/processed/iacofi_clean.csv — 4,862 rows × 222 columns

Score Construction & Validation

notebooks/02_score_construction.ipynb

Implements the OECD/INFE composite financial literacy score (0–21) exactly following the international methodology. Uses NaN-aware scoring so unanswered questions do not inflate scores.

Three Dimensions

Knowledge (0–7)

7 factual questions on inflation, interest rates, simple & compound interest, risk-return trade-off, and diversification.

Mean: 5.02 ± 1.56

Behaviour (0–9)

9 binary items: budgeting, saving habits, ability to cover financial shocks, paying bills on time, setting financial goals, monitoring spending.

Mean: 4.81 ± 1.98

Attitudes (0–5)

5 Likert items on future financial orientation, satisfaction, goal clarity, worry levels, and sense of financial control.

Mean: 1.77 ± 1.31

Validation Checks

  • Inter-dimension correlations: Knowledge–Behaviour 0.150, Behaviour–Attitudes 0.281, Knowledge–Attitudes −0.015 → dimensions are related but distinct, confirming the composite score is theoretically valid.

Notable: The Youth Paradox

Adults aged 18–29 score 10.25 / 21 — the lowest of any age group, even below the 70–79 cohort (11.66). This contradicts the intuition that younger people are more financially savvy. It reflects lack of real-world financial exposure rather than cognitive decline.

04

Predictive Modelling & Clustering

notebooks/04_model.ipynb

Trains baseline supervised models to predict financial vulnerability (binary: below median literacy score) from 8 demographic features, and uses unsupervised K-Means clustering to discover hidden subgroups.

Problem Setup

  • Target: vulnerable = 1 if literacy score < 12 (median) — 41.5% of sample
  • Features: Gender, Region, Education, Income, Work status, Internet access, Urban/rural, Age
  • Training set: 3,173 rows (after dropping rows with any missing feature/target)
  • 80/20 train-test split, stratified; LR features scaled with StandardScaler

Baseline Model Results

ModelFeaturesCV AccuracyTest Accuracy
Logistic Regression 8 demographic 58.2% 57.8%
Random Forest 8 demographic 54.9% ~55%

Modest accuracy is expected: demographics are distal predictors of financial literacy — a high-bias signal. The LR model achieves recall of only 5% on the vulnerable class, showing demographics alone cannot reliably flag at-risk individuals.

05

Feature Engineering & Gradient Boosting

notebooks/05_feature_engineering..ipynb

The central insight notebook. Constructs 7 behavioural composite features from raw survey items and shows they dramatically outperform demographics alone. Final model: Gradient Boosting at 70.1% CV accuracy.

Engineered Features

  • Financial Resilience Index (0–3) r = 0.44
    Can cover financial shock (qf4) + money left over (qs2_4) + has savings goal (qf13)
  • Product Breadth r = 0.37
    Count of financial products currently held (savings, insurance, investments, etc.)
  • Budget Behaviour Score (0–6) r = 0.32
    Count of active budgeting methods used (qf2_1–qf2_6)
  • Active Saver Flag r = 0.27
    Whether respondent has any active saving vehicle (binary)
  • Self-rated Knowledge r = 0.20
    Reversed scale: 5 = very high knowledge, 1 = very low
  • Digital Engagement Score r = 0.03
    Mean usage frequency of digital financial services (weak predictor)
  • Age Squared r = 0.05
    Captures potential non-linear age effect (weak — dropped from final model)

Final Feature Set

13 features: 8 demographic + 5 behavioural (resilience_index, product_breadth, budget_score, active_saver, self_knowledge). Training set: 3,026 rows.

Central Finding

Adding behavioural features lifts accuracy from ~55% → 70% (+15 percentage points). The top 3 predictors are all behavioural, not demographic. What people do financially matters more than who they are. A single feature — the financial-resilience index — explains more variance than every demographic variable combined (r = 0.44).

Limitations
  • Cross-sectional data — associations only, no causal inference
  • Income non-response 31% — likely non-random, may bias income analyses
  • Self-reported behaviour — possible social-desirability bias
  • Coarse geography — only 5 macro-regions for ISTAT enrichment
  • Attitudes sub-score — mean 1.77/5, very low and with unusual K–A correlation (−0.015)
